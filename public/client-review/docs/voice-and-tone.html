Voice and Tone

Writing rules, banned phrases, and voice guidance.

Real Wealth — Voice and Tone

Real Wealth's voice is warm, considered, and plain-spoken. It is the voice of a senior planner sitting across the table from a client who matters to them — not a brochure, not a chatbot, not a compliance form.

The lead-magnet app is the first time a prospect hears this voice. It has to sound like the person they will later meet. If the form sounds like a different company from the one that takes the call, we have lost the lead at the handover.

---

The three notes

1. Warm

We like people. We are interested in their lives. Our language is friendly without being casual, personal without being familiar. We address the user as you, never the client.

Sounds like: "Tell us a bit about the people you plan for." Not: "Please select the relevant family structure."

2. Considered

We choose our words. We are senior, experienced advisers — we do not need to oversell, use hype words, or fill space. A short, well-chosen sentence does more than three clever ones. The serif face carries some of this weight; the copy carries the rest.

Sounds like: "A few people have found this one useful to think about." Not: "Unlock the #1 secret top advisers don't want you to know."

3. Plain-spoken

We do not hide behind jargon. Tax, pensions, and estates are complicated enough without us making them worse. When a technical term is necessary, we define it on the same line. When it is not necessary, we remove it.

Sounds like: "If your estate is over £2m, some of the usual inheritance tax reliefs start to taper away." Not: "Estates exceeding the £2m threshold experience RNRB taper erosion at £1 per £2 above threshold."

What we do not do

No hype

No words like unlock, supercharge, transform, guaranteed, proven, revolutionary. These words make regulated firms sound unregulated. They are also regulatory red flags under Consumer Duty and FCA financial promotion rules.

No fear appeals

We do not scare people into a call. "You could be losing thousands!" or "Act now before it's too late" belong to a different kind of firm. Our provocations are factual and quiet — "This is a £X question for households like yours. Worth a conversation."

No false urgency

No countdowns. No "only 3 spots left this week". No artificial scarcity. Our calls are genuinely scarce in a different way — a senior planner has limited capacity — but we do not dramatise it.

No advice

We never give advice in the app. The app is a guidance tool in the FCA sense — it surfaces topics for discussion. We do not say "you should", "we recommend", or "you ought to". We say "worth talking about", "this might apply to you", "a planner can help you think through this".

No pity-the-customer

We do not call tax "a nightmare", decisions "scary", or future planning "daunting". That frame puts us above the user. Our frame is "we see these questions all the time — here's how we help".

---

Sentence rhythm

  • Prefer short sentences. Under 20 words is a good target. A long sentence is fine when the content warrants it — complex ideas sometimes need length — but three long sentences in a row become a wall.
  • Begin a sentence with and or but when it helps the rhythm. We are not writing a legal document.
  • Use the serif face (gelica) for questions and provocations to slow the reader down. Use the sans face (area-normal) for options to speed them up. The typography does work that the copy does not have to do alone.
  • Read every line aloud before shipping. If you would not say it to a client, do not write it.

---

The question voice

Questions are the most important copy in the app. They are written as if a planner were asking them across a coffee table.

Good:

  • "Tell us a bit about you."
  • "Who else are you planning for?"
  • "Do you have any money set aside in a business?"
  • "If everything went well, what does retirement look like?"

Less good:

  • "Please indicate your marital status."
  • "Do you own a business (Y/N)?"
  • "State your retirement plans."

The "less good" versions are not wrong — they are just transactional. They tell the user they are filling in a form. The "good" versions tell the user they are in a conversation.

---

The provocation voice

Provocations are the app's quiet killer. They are short, specific, and never salesy.

Provocation patterns

The customer quote

Real-sounding, lightly edited, attributed to a first name and role.

"We had no idea the family home could create a tax problem this big."
— Andrew, retired engineer, Cheshire
The specific fact

A tax threshold, a rule change, a magnitude. Always sourced, always current.

The £2m cliff. Your residence nil-rate band starts tapering above £2m — losing £1 of relief for every £2 above. Something planners can usually address, worth a conversation.
The magnitude flag

A number that communicates the scale of the topic without quantifying the user personally.

A £40k/year decision. Clients who defer annual pension contributions can miss out on roughly £40k of tax relief over a typical career. Not advice — a good topic for a call.
The gentle provocation

Tone-matched to a softer moment — e.g. family, protection, legacy.

A note from us. Most couples we meet haven't made lasting powers of attorney yet. It's a quiet thing until it isn't. Worth half an hour with us to put one in place.

Provocation rules

  • Never longer than two sentences of body copy plus headline.
  • Never state the user's specific figure back to them — we can say "households in your band", not "your £2m estate".
  • Always end with a soft conversational prompt: "worth a conversation", "a planner can help you think through this", "we can talk about the trade-offs". Never "click here", "act now", "book today".
  • Each provocation must be approved by compliance before shipping. Tag each one with a compliance_status in the JSON library.

The final page voice

The final page is where we ask for the call. The tone is warm, honest, and uncrowded.

Aspiration echo (top of the final page)

Echo the user's aspiration back to them in quiet gelica. This is the single most emotional moment in the app.

"The life you're planning for: mornings with grandchildren, an honest retirement, time you actually own."
(Generated by blending keywords from the aspiration question into a hand-curated set of templates — see the prototype spec.)

Your considered list

"Here are the things worth thinking about, based on what you've told us. None of this is advice yet — it's a shortlist of conversations that would be valuable for someone in your situation."

Then the list of 3–6 items from the provocations they saw. Each item re-presented as a prompt: "Inheritance tax on estates over £2m", "Pension access and tax after 6 April 2027", etc.

The illustrative charts

"Illustrative example." (uppercase, brand-orange, 13px, above each chart title.)
"These are not projections of your personal numbers. They show the shape of the question for a typical household. Your numbers, your plan, your answer — that's a conversation."

Error messages

  • Never blame the user. Never say "invalid input" or "error".
  • Point at the fix, not the problem.
  • Human words: "Looks like we need a valid email", "Mind giving that one another try?"

---

Consent and data copy

Consent copy must be plain English. The consent tick on the final page uses this language:

"I'd like Real Wealth to contact me about booking a call and to send occasional planning insights. I can unsubscribe any time."

This is one sentence, in the user's voice, with a clear opt-out. It is not a legalese block. The privacy notice link sits below in small type for those who want the detail.

---

Vulnerability and Consumer Duty

Under Consumer Duty, the voice must be safe for someone who is emotionally vulnerable (bereavement, divorce, ill-health), financially anxious, or cognitively tired.

  • No shock tactics. No dramatic figures without context.
  • No words that imply blame: "should have", "ought to have", "it's too late to…".
  • Always offer a way out — the user can close the tab without guilt and return later. Progress is saved.
  • If a question touches sensitive ground (e.g. health, family bereavement, separation), the micro-copy under the question acknowledges it: "We ask because it can really shape what a plan looks like. Skip if you'd rather."
