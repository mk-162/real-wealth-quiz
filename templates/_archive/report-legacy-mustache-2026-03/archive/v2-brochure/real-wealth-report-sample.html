Five things worth a conversation.

The shape of this list will feel familiar to anyone in their early fifties with a household income in six figures. Here’s what a planner would want to dig into first.

  1. 1. The £100k income-tax trap

    The 60% effective marginal band between £100k and £125k catches most high earners by surprise. Pension contributions are often the cheapest way through it.

  2. 2. Pension carry-forward

    You can potentially contribute up to £180k across three tax years. Whether that’s worth it depends on your income shape and what “enough” looks like at 67.

  3. 3. Estate planning, gently

    At £1m+ estate with succession still open, inheritance tax starts to matter. A conversation about where the money goes before it’s inherited is usually cheaper than after.

  4. 4. Joint-partner allowances

    Couples with differing incomes often miss the easy ones: pension topping-up between you, ISA maximisation, and the marriage / transferable allowance.

  5. 5. The protection question no one asks

    What happens to household income if one of you can’t work for six months? For a mid-career couple with a mortgage, this is almost always the biggest under-covered area.

Things we didn’t ask — but noticed.

You didn’t tell us about these directly — but based on the rest of the conversation, they’re the quiet ones a planner would bring to a first meeting.

  • 01

    Wills and LPAs

    Households at the £1m+ estate level without an up-to-date Will and Lasting Power of Attorney are surprisingly common. The cost of not having them is disproportionate.

  • 02

    Employer benefits

    Most large employers offer death-in-service, group income protection, and pension matching. Many of these quietly go under-used.

  • 03

    State pension forecast

    A few minutes on gov.uk surfaces any NI gaps. Plugging them in your fifties is materially cheaper than plugging them at sixty-six.

  • 04

    Investment fees

    A 0.5% difference on a £500k portfolio compounds to over £100k across 20 years. Worth a one-time audit.

A planner’s read — three lenses.

This isn’t advice yet — it’s the shape of a first conversation. Each block is something a planner would want to understand before suggesting anything specific.

Insight

Your income band is where pension contributions stop being a “nice idea” and start being a material saving on tax.

Context

Above £100k, personal allowance tapers. The effective marginal rate between £100k–£125k is 60%. Pension contributions reduce adjusted net income and can restore the allowance.

One move

Model this year’s contribution against next year’s bonus shape — often more tax-efficient to contribute from a bonus than from salary.

Insight

Estates in the £1–2m band are where inheritance planning becomes materially useful without needing a complicated structure.

Context

Residence and transferable nil-rate bands together give a couple up to £1m free of IHT. Above that, 40% applies. The cheapest moves are usually policies in trust or gifts made within the seven-year rule.

One move

Draft a simple “if we were hit by a bus” letter — all accounts, advisers, key people. An hour of work that saves months of distress.

Insight

Your income is the asset that matters most before retirement — and it’s often the least protected.

Context

A household relying on one or two incomes typically needs life cover at 10–12x salary, and critical-illness cover at 3–5x, to replace income through a major event.

One move

Check your employer’s death-in-service and group income protection before buying anything new — often the baseline is surprisingly good.
