# Employer pension contributions as a business extraction tool

For owner-managed companies, employer pension contributions are a deductible business expense. The company claims corporation tax relief on the contribution — saving 25% for a company paying the main rate. The contribution does not attract income tax or National Insurance at the time it is made. The pension funds grow free of income and capital gains tax within the wrapper, and the money is eventually drawn down in retirement, typically at a lower marginal rate than the rate at which it would have been taxed as a dividend or salary during working life.

The comparison with dividend extraction is material. On a £60,000 employer pension contribution, a 25% corporation-tax-paying company saves £15,000 in tax. The owner also avoids the income tax and NI that would have applied to an equivalent dividend — saving a further £10,000–£20,000 depending on their personal tax position. The all-in advantage over a pure dividend extraction, at the point of contribution, can be 30%–40% of the sum involved. Very few business owners with one or zero pension pots are using this lever deliberately.

The annual allowance applies — the standard limit is £60,000 per year, reduced if the owner also draws a salary above the tapered allowance threshold. Carry-forward from previous years can significantly increase the available headroom in a good year. The right time to have this conversation is usually before a profitable year-end, not after — once the corporation tax position is settled, the window to shelter that profit into a pension has closed for that year.
