# The £100,000 income tax trap

Between £100,000 and £125,140, the UK tax system quietly withdraws your personal allowance at a rate of £1 for every £2 you earn above the threshold. Combined with the 40% higher-rate band and National Insurance, the effective marginal rate on income in this range works out at around 60% — not the 40% most people in this bracket assume they're paying.

The maths is worth seeing once. A £15,000 pay rise from £100,000 to £115,000 costs roughly £9,000 in income tax and NI. If you have two young children and access to tax-free childcare, you may also lose up to £10,000 of childcare entitlement across that same band — meaning a £15,000 increase in headline pay can leave you materially worse off. It is a band, not a cliff: the effective 60% rate starts at £100,000 and ends at £125,140, where the full personal allowance is gone and the rate drops back.

The most common approach for reducing the exposure is a pension contribution or salary sacrifice arrangement that brings adjusted net income back below £100,000. Most people in this band have not set one up — often because no one has shown them the numbers in this form. That tends to be where the first planning conversation lands.
