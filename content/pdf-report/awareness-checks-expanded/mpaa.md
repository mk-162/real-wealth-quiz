# The money purchase annual allowance — the limit you may have already triggered

Once you begin drawing taxable income from a defined-contribution pension — even a small, exploratory withdrawal — your annual pension contribution allowance drops from £60,000 to £10,000. This reduced limit is called the Money Purchase Annual Allowance, or MPAA. It applies to contributions to all money purchase (DC) pension schemes — including any new employer pension — and it does not unwind. Once triggered, the lower limit applies for the rest of your life.

The trigger is specific: it is the first withdrawal of taxable income from a flexi-access drawdown arrangement. Taking the tax-free lump sum (the 25% PCLS) alone does not trigger the MPAA, provided the remaining fund stays in a designated drawdown pot rather than being moved to flexi-access. But any taxable income drawn from flexi-access drawdown — whether £500 or £50,000 — crystallises the lower allowance immediately. A surprising number of people who took a small withdrawal in their early 50s "just to see how it worked" subsequently discovered they had permanently reduced their future contribution capacity by 83%.

The consequence tends to matter most for people who are still working, still earning, and still benefiting from employer pension contributions — since the employer contributions also count against the MPAA. If your employer contributes, say, £8,000 per year and you want to also contribute personally, you may already be at or near the £10,000 ceiling. It is worth checking your drawdown history before assuming the standard annual allowance still applies to you.
