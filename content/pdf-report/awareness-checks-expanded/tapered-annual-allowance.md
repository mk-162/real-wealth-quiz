# The tapered annual allowance — how the pension contribution limit shrinks at high income

The standard pension annual allowance is £60,000 per year. But above £260,000 of adjusted income — broadly, total income including employer pension contributions — that allowance begins to taper downwards. For every £2 of adjusted income above £260,000, the allowance reduces by £1, all the way down to a minimum floor of £10,000 per year, reached at £360,000 of adjusted income. Carry-forward from the previous three years can supplement this reduced allowance if unused capacity exists.

At £300,000 of adjusted income, the effective pension annual allowance is £40,000 rather than £60,000. At £360,000 it is £10,000. Contributing more than your allowance — even inadvertently, including employer contributions made without considering the taper — triggers an annual allowance charge that claws back the relief at your marginal rate. The charge is assessed on the individual's tax return, not automatically deducted at source, which means it can arrive as a large unexpected bill if the contributions haven't been planned against the reduced limit.

The most common practical conversation at this income level concerns carry-forward. If unused allowance from the previous three tax years is available, it can be added to the tapered figure in the current year — but the three-year window rolls forward each April. Allowance from a year where the taper was not triggered is worth more than a year where it was. Many people at this income level have never systematically tracked their carry-forward position, and the discovery that a meaningful sum could have been sheltered in pension tax relief — and is now gone — tends to sharpen the interest in the exercise for future years.
