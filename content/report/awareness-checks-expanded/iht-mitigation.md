# Inheritance tax — what the planning toolkit actually looks like

At an estate of £1m or more, the default inheritance tax position is 40% on everything above the nil-rate band. That is £325,000 per person, or up to £500,000 if you qualify for the residence nil-rate band and are leaving a home to direct descendants. On a £2m estate, the liability is typically £540,000–£700,000 before any planning — and the nil-rate bands are frozen to April 2030, so the real-terms bill rises every year.

The planning toolkit has four main components, each with its own timeline. Gifting within the seven-year rule removes assets from the estate gradually — the full exemption applies if you survive seven years. Trust structures (discretionary trusts, life-interest trusts) can remove assets while preserving some control over how they are used. Business Relief-qualifying investments — including AIM portfolios — can become IHT-exempt after two years. The residence nil-rate band applies where the family home passes to direct descendants, adding up to £175,000 per person to the nil-rate threshold; above £2m of estate it begins to taper away (see the RNRB section below). The 2024 Budget changed several of these rules, particularly around pension inheritance from April 2027.

In our experience, families with two to five years of structured planning typically reduce the bill by £150,000–£400,000 against the default position. The conversation is not about avoiding tax — it is about making sure the people you intend to benefit actually receive what you intended. The earlier the planning starts, the more of the toolkit remains available.
