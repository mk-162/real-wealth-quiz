# Income protection — the gap between employer sick pay and what the household needs

Statutory Sick Pay in the UK is currently £116.75 per week — approximately £505 per month. It is available for up to 28 weeks. Many employers offer enhanced sick pay above this level, but the typical scheme pays full salary for one to six months, then half pay, then drops to SSP, then nothing. For most households with a mortgage and monthly outgoings of £2,000 or more, the half-pay line is already below essential spending. By the time SSP kicks in, the gap between income and outgoings is significant.

The risk this creates is common but underestimated. Serious illness or injury severe enough to prevent work for more than six months is considerably more likely than early death before retirement — which means the financial exposure from a lack of income protection is often larger than the exposure from a lack of life cover. Despite this, income protection insurance is far less widely held than life insurance. A policy that pays, say, £3,000–£4,000 per month after a six-month deferred period, until the policyholder returns to work or reaches state pension age, typically costs £30–£60 per month for a non-smoker in good health in their late thirties or forties.

Income protection premiums are directly linked to age and health status at the time of application. A policy taken at 35 is cheaper and typically easier to obtain than the equivalent policy at 45, and materially cheaper than one taken at 50. The deferred period — the waiting time before the policy pays out — is a key variable: a longer deferred period (six months rather than one) significantly reduces the premium, and can be aligned with the length of the existing employer sick-pay provision so there is no unnecessary overlap.
