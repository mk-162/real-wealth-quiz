# Financial alignment in couples — the planning conversation that most couples haven't had

Research consistently finds that couples who have a shared, explicit understanding of their financial position — where they are, where they are heading, and what the plan is — report significantly higher satisfaction with their finances than couples who leave money largely to one partner or handle it purely reactively. The conversation is not primarily about the numbers; it is about having a shared picture of what the numbers are working toward.

The practical gaps that tend to matter most are not complex. A shared retirement picture — what age, what income, what lifestyle — is rarely as aligned as either partner assumes until the question is asked directly. Decisions about gifting to children often produce genuine differences of opinion that neither partner had surfaced. One partner typically knows where the accounts, policies, and passwords are; the other typically does not. These are not character failures — they are the natural result of households dividing labour in ways that work day to day but leave one person significantly more exposed if something changes.

In our experience, the most consistently valuable part of a first planning meeting with a couple is not the tax analysis or the retirement projection — it is the fifteen minutes spent establishing what both partners actually want, compared side by side. It tends to produce a more useful and honest briefing for the planning work that follows, and it resolves several assumptions each partner had made about the other that turn out to be wrong in ways that matter. The financial plan that comes out of that conversation tends to stick better than one built on one partner's answers alone.
