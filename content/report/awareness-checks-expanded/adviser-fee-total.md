# The total cost of advice — seeing all three layers as one number

The cost of advised investing has three components: the adviser's ongoing fee, the platform or custody charge, and the fund's ongoing charges figure (OCF). These are typically disclosed separately, at different points in the onboarding process, and often in different documents. Most investors have seen the adviser fee — quoted as a percentage of assets under management, typically 0.5%–1.0% — but many have not added the other two layers onto it. The total usually lands somewhere between 0.7% and 2.0% per year, and the difference across that range is material over time.

On a £500,000 portfolio with a 1.5% all-in cost versus a 0.8% all-in cost, the difference in terminal value after twenty years at 5% gross growth is roughly £140,000. That is not an argument that 1.5% is wrong — an active tax planning service, comprehensive protection review, and regular rebalancing may deliver more than that difference in value. The FCA's Consumer Duty rules, introduced in 2023, require firms to demonstrate that the outcomes they deliver justify the total cost, not just the adviser component.

The question worth asking your current adviser is simple: what is the total all-in cost across adviser, platform, and fund charges as a single percentage? If the answer is not immediately available, it is worth asking for it in writing. A well-run advice relationship should be able to produce this number promptly. Understanding the total is not a prelude to a conversation about whether advice is worth it — it is the starting point for having that conversation on accurate information rather than incomplete disclosure.
