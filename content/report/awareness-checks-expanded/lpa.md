# Lasting Power of Attorney — the legal gap that costs the most to discover late

A Lasting Power of Attorney is a legal document that authorises someone you choose to manage your financial affairs, or your health and welfare decisions, if you lose the mental capacity to do so yourself. There are two separate documents — one for property and financial affairs, one for health and welfare — and both need to be registered with the Office of the Public Guardian before they can be used. The current registration fee is £82 per LPA. Most solicitors charge £300–£600 to prepare both.

Without registered LPAs in place, no family member has the legal authority to access your bank accounts, pay your mortgage, or make healthcare decisions on your behalf if capacity is lost. The alternative — applying to the Court of Protection for a deputyship — typically takes six months or more and costs £3,000–£5,000 in professional fees, plus an annual supervision fee thereafter. It is also more limited in scope than a well-drafted LPA, and the person appointed may not be who you would have chosen.
