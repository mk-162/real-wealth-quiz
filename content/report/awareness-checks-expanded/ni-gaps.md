# National Insurance gaps — the state pension shortfall most people haven't checked

The full new state pension — currently £11,502 per year — requires 35 qualifying years of National Insurance contributions. Fewer than 35 qualifying years means a proportionally reduced pension for life. Gaps are most common among people who spent time self-employed on low earnings, took a career break, worked abroad, or moved between employment and self-employment without tracking their record. The Pension Forecast tool at gov.uk takes about 90 seconds to check and shows the current position clearly.

Voluntary Class 3 NI contributions currently cost around £824 per year. Each qualifying year purchased adds approximately £328 per year to the state pension — a payback period of around two and a half years, after which the uplift continues for the rest of your life. For most people in reasonable health, filling genuine NI gaps is one of the highest-return financial decisions available, with returns of 10–15 times the cost over a typical retirement. The normal look-back window is six years, meaning gaps older than six years cannot typically be filled.

Around 60% of people in their late 40s and 50s have never checked their NI record. The most common surprises are gaps from periods of self-employment on low income, years spent caring for children without claiming Child Benefit (which carries NI credits), or time working abroad. If your state pension forecast is below £11,502, understanding why and whether any gaps are fillable is a relatively simple exercise with a potentially very material outcome.
