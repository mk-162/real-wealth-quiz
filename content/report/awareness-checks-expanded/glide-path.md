# Pension investment risk close to retirement — the default that may not fit your plan

Most workplace DC pension schemes invest contributions in a "default fund" — designed to suit a typical member without requiring active investment decisions. Before 2015, most default funds were built on the assumption that members would buy an annuity at retirement, and they gradually shifted from equities to bonds and cash as the target date approached (a process called "lifestyling"). After the 2015 pension freedoms, most schemes redesigned their defaults to assume drawdown rather than annuity purchase — which means they retain a higher proportion of equities for longer.

This is not wrong in itself. For a 40-year-old, equity risk in a pension is appropriate and expected. For a 60-year-old five years from planned retirement with a specific income target in mind, the picture is different. A 25% equity fall in the two years before retirement is very different in its consequences from the same fall twenty years earlier, because you have less time to recover and you may be drawing on the fund before markets recover. Whether the current default fund is appropriately de-risked for your timeline is a question that most near-retirees have never been prompted to ask.

The answer depends on your plan — specifically, whether you intend to take your pension as drawdown, as an annuity, or some combination. A drawdown strategy may justify staying in equities longer; an annuity purchase does not. The question is worth checking against your current default fund's investment fact sheet, which shows the current allocation and any automatic de-risking that is or is not in place. In our experience it is rare for this to have been actively reviewed in the five years before a planned retirement date.
