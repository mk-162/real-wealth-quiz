# Emergency fund sizing — why three months is often not enough

The widely quoted rule of thumb is three months of essential spending held in accessible cash. For a dual-income household with no dependants and no mortgage, that figure is probably adequate. For most other households — one primary earner, dependants, a mortgage, and limited alternative income — three months is typically undersized.

The reason is that the most common financial emergencies are not brief. A serious illness can mean four to six months off work before long-term sick pay arrangements or income protection activates. A redundancy from a senior role — particularly in a specialist field — often takes longer than three months to resolve. A property emergency, a gap between tenancies for buy-to-let landlords, or an unexpected tax demand can each absorb several months of essential outgoings in a single event. At three months of cover, any of these forces the sale of longer-term investments, often at short notice and occasionally at a bad point in the market.

Most financial planners use different benchmarks depending on household type: six months for an employed single-income household with dependants; nine to twelve months for the self-employed, commission-based earners, or business owners whose income is variable. The precise number is judgement, not formula — it depends on how quickly the household could access alternative income, how stable the current income is, and what the alternative sources of short-term liquidity look like. Cash held in emergency reserves earns less than invested assets, so the buffer carries a cost; the question is whether that cost is proportionate to the risk of not having it.
