# The residence nil-rate band taper above £2 million

The residence nil-rate band adds up to £175,000 per person — £350,000 for a married couple — to the IHT-free threshold, provided the main home passes to direct descendants. But above £2 million of total estate, this relief begins to taper away: £1 of RNRB is lost for every £2 of estate above the £2m threshold. At £2.35m per person, the residence nil-rate band is fully withdrawn. For a married couple, the combined taper cliff runs to £4.7m.

The practical impact is significant. A family with a £2.5m estate may assume they have the full £500,000 RNRB available; in practice they have lost most of it, and the IHT bill is £200,000–£500,000 higher than a simple nil-rate band calculation would suggest. The £2m threshold has not risen with inflation — meaning more families fall inside the taper every year without any change in their real wealth. From April 2027, DC pensions are expected to count toward the £2m threshold, which will pull more estates into the taper without the underlying asset mix changing.

The planning conversation is typically about keeping the estate below the taper threshold, or reducing it far enough that the RNRB is at least partially restored. The tools are usually the same as for IHT planning generally — timed gifting, Business Relief-qualifying investments, trust structures — but the RNRB taper adds a specific target figure to the exercise. In our experience it is one of the less-understood parts of IHT planning, and one of the more material ones for families in the £2m–£3m range.
