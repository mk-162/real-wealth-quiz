# The all-in cost of investing — why the quoted figure is rarely the full figure

Investment costs have three layers: the adviser's ongoing fee (typically 0.5%–1.0% per year), the platform charge (typically 0.15%–0.35%), and the fund's ongoing charges figure or OCF (typically 0.05%–0.9%, depending on the fund type and style). These are rarely quoted together as a single number. Most investors have seen the adviser fee, and some have seen the platform charge, but very few have been shown all three added up into one annual cost figure.

The compounding effect of the difference matters more on larger portfolios and over longer time horizons. On a £500,000 portfolio growing at 5% gross for twenty years, the difference between a 1.0% and a 0.4% all-in annual cost is approximately £70,000 of terminal value. For a £1m portfolio the same arithmetic produces roughly £140,000. That is not an argument against paying for advice — it is an argument for knowing what you are paying for all three layers combined, and for being confident the value delivered justifies the total. Consumer Duty, introduced in 2023, requires advisers to demonstrate value against the total cost, not just their own line.

The most common gap we find is not that one layer is too high but that nobody has ever added them up for the client as a single figure. Asking for the total all-in cost — adviser plus platform plus fund charges — as one annual percentage and one pound figure is a reasonable and standard question. If the answer is not immediately available, that in itself is useful information.
