# Will currency — the document that drifts out of date without announcing it

A will is a snapshot of your intentions at a point in time. Marriage, divorce, the birth of children or grandchildren, the purchase of a new home, a significant business stake, the death of a named beneficiary, or an estrangement — any of these changes the picture your will was written to reflect, often without the document being updated to match. A will drafted five or more years ago in a different household or financial situation may distribute your estate in ways that no longer reflect your wishes.

The problems most commonly seen are not dramatic failures but quiet mismatches: an old will that leaves everything to a former partner because the decree absolute post-dated the signing; a will that names an executor who has since died or become estranged; a will that does not reflect the addition of stepchildren or grandchildren; or a will that predates the purchase of a property and doesn't direct it clearly. In the most serious cases the mismatch produces a seven-figure dispute between family members. More commonly it produces an estate that takes a year to resolve instead of three months.

The standard advice is to re-read a will every five years and following any significant life event. Updating a simple will costs a few hundred pounds. A well-drafted will that reflects the current family structure, accounts for the residence nil-rate band where it applies, and appoints an appropriate executor is one of the most cost-effective legal documents available. If the current will has not been reviewed since a material change in circumstances, this is worth doing before any of the other planning conversations on this page.
