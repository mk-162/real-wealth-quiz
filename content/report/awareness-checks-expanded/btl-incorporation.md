# Buy-to-let properties and the incorporation question

Since 2017, the rules for buy-to-let properties held personally have changed significantly. Section 24 of the Finance Act 2015 replaced full mortgage interest relief with a flat 20% tax credit for individual landlords. For basic-rate taxpayers, the practical effect is limited. For higher and additional-rate taxpayers with mortgaged properties, the effect can be substantial: the tax is now calculated on gross rental income, not income net of mortgage interest, with only a 20% credit applied afterwards. It is possible — in the right combination of high mortgage and thin yield — to pay tax on income you did not actually receive.

Properties held within a limited company are not subject to Section 24. The company pays corporation tax (25% at the main rate) on net profit after mortgage interest, which restores the deductibility of financing costs. This is the basis of the "should I incorporate?" question that many buy-to-let landlords with two or more properties are now asking. The answer is not straightforward. Transferring a personally-held property into a company triggers stamp duty land tax on the purchase price and capital gains tax on any accrued gain, unless a formal business partnership structure existed before the transfer. The one-off costs of incorporation for a typical two-property landlord are usually £5,000–£15,000.

The calculation is therefore whether the ongoing annual tax saving from holding inside a company outweighs the one-off transfer cost, over your expected holding period. For a higher-rate taxpayer with heavily mortgaged properties and a long intended holding period, the answer is sometimes yes, sometimes no — it depends on the specific numbers. This is a conversation that runs best jointly with a tax accountant who knows the portfolio, rather than as a general question.
