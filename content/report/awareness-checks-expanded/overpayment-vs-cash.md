# Mortgage overpayment versus cash saving — comparing the after-tax return

If your mortgage rate is 5%, overpaying the mortgage gives you a guaranteed after-tax return of 5%. To match that return in a taxable savings account, a basic-rate taxpayer would need to earn around 6.3% gross; a higher-rate taxpayer would need around 8.3% gross. Very few cash savings accounts currently offer returns at those levels. For households with mortgage debt and surplus cash above their emergency fund, the comparison between overpayment and savings is often less obvious than it first appears, and the arithmetic usually favours overpayment.

The numbers are concrete. On a £300,000 mortgage at 5%, overpaying £500 per month saves approximately £27,000 in interest over the mortgage term and shortens the remaining repayment period by around three years. The same £500 per month in a cash savings account at 4.5% gross, taxed at 40% for a higher-rate taxpayer, earns an effective 2.7% — generating a materially lower cumulative return. The gap closes if rates change significantly, which is why this is a live calculation rather than a permanent rule.

Two factors complicate the straightforward comparison. First, most mortgage products limit overpayments to 10% of the outstanding balance per year without triggering early redemption penalties — worth checking before overpaying significantly. Second, money inside the house is not as accessible as money in a savings account: once an overpayment is made, you cannot retrieve it easily without remortgaging. The balance between return and liquidity is the planning question, and the right answer depends on the size of the emergency fund already in place and how far from the mortgage term end you are.
